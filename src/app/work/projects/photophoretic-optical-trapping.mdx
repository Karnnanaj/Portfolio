## Overview

Developed a photophoretic optical trapping system utilizing a high-power (100 mW) 532 nm continuous-wave laser. Unlike traditional dielectric optical tweezers that rely on electromagnetic gradient forces, this system leverages photophoresis—where non-uniform heating of absorbing particles in air creates a stable thermal pressure differential, enabling particle confinement.

## Technical Architecture

The optical train employs a sophisticated beam expansion and focusing geometry consisting of:
- **Laser Source**: 100 mW 532 nm continuous-wave laser
- **Beam Expansion & Focusing Optics**: Two precision lenses with focal lengths of 50 mm and 6 mm
- **Optical Design**: Optimized to create high-intensity gradient at the focal plane (calculated beam waist ~6 mm)

## Trapping Mechanism

### Photophoresis vs Traditional Tweezers

Traditional optical tweezers rely on dielectric constant gradients to trap neutral dielectric particles. Photophoretic trapping operates through a fundamentally different mechanism:

1. **Non-uniform Heating**: The focused laser beam heats absorbing particles non-uniformly
2. **Thermal Gradient**: Creates a temperature gradient across the particle surface
3. **Pressure Differential**: Asymmetric thermal accommodation creates net force toward cooler regions
4. **Particle Containment**: Stable confinement maintained by thermal pressure walls

### Trapping Targets

Carbon clusters ejected from a substrate composed of:
- Ethanol base medium
- Whiteboard marker ink (carbon source)

These materials provide strong optical absorption at 532 nm while maintaining structural coherence suitable for extended trapping periods.

## Experimental Results

The trapped particle is observed as a stationary, high-intensity spot located approximately **6 mm from the final lens**, corresponding precisely to the calculated beam waist of the focused laser. This confirms theoretical predictions and demonstrates successful photophoretic confinement.

## Recognition & Impact

This project was successfully exhibited at the **National Photonics Symposium (NPS 2026)** hosted at the International School of Photonics, CUSAT. The demonstration showcased the viability of photophoretic trapping as an alternative to conventional optical tweezers, particularly valuable for:
- Absorbing particle manipulation (carbon, metal oxides)
- Temperature-controlled environments
- Applications requiring non-contact particle handling in air media
